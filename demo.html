First Things First

While you may know me as John Harrison, I actually go by my middle name, Ragan. I'm an electrical engineering student at North Carolina State University in Raleigh, North Carolina. In addition to studying math and science, I also regularly work to widen my vocabulary in Mandarin Chinese.

HTML, Adobe, and Engineering

Yes, you heard right! It's an interesting combination, I know. While I'd love to spend all of my free time analyzing logic circuits or coding in C++, my creative calling often requires me to spend at least two hours a week pursuing more leisurely activities. For me, this includes breaking out the Wocam tablet, listening to the newest Tycho album, and drawing whatever comes to mind.

Okay. But, why Chinese?

For nearly three years now, I have been on a long, fulfilling journey to become fluent in Mandarin Chinese. The reason? I've always been intrigued by the pictorial nature of the language. The earliest versions of Chinese characters were literally cave drawings. As time went on, these evolved into smaller, more compact images that contained a variety of meanings when rearranged. The uniqueness, history, and graphical nature of the language is reason enough for me to include it as a part of my daily studies.

Past, Present, and Future Endeavours

Virtual Reality
The Matrix of Design
At the beginning of this year, I became employed at my university's virtual reality lab. Being able to experience VR technology and its many applications is surreal to say the least. During my first month working in the lab, I assisted an architectural firm as they presented blueprints for an observatory that will be built in Beijing. As I helped them add the final touches to their 3D simulations, it really struck me how this technology makes the issue of communication almost nonexistent. Being able to create complex 3D models and then have them displayed to an audience via Google Blocks is incredibly powerful, especially from a designer's perspective.
3D Printing and Modeling
Always Choose Resin
For the past four years, I've gained valuable knowledge of 3D printers by working with, repairing, and even building them. From my experience, a Formlabs resin-based printer will always take the cake in terms of quality. Despite its lengthy print time, it always prioritizes detail, making it my go-to printer for important projects and deadlines. If I'm willing to sacrifice some detail to increase the speed, I can't go wrong queuing my design in a Lulzbot mini. Despite the individual strands of filament decorating the sides of a print, every project is created quickly with surprising accuracy.
Blender and Autodesk and Cura, Oh My!
Whenever I'm waiting for someone's print to finish up, I often get straight to work putting the final touches on my own. Undoubtedly, the best programs to do this are Blender and Autodesk. With their wide selection of tools, patterns, and other styling devices, there's never been a
moment where I couldn't convert my ideas into a 3D render using this software.
Digital Design
More Adobe, You Say?
It's no secret among those who know me that I adore Adobe's products. During my freshman year of high school I was first introduced to Illustrator and Photoshop. The layout, accessibility, and diverse tool set had me hooked at first pen stroke. Later that year, one of my Christmas gifts was a one-year subscription to the creative cloud. That subscription has not been cancelled once in six years. My favorite applications of the suite are undoubtedly Illustrator, Photoshop, and InDesign. That being said, Premier Pro and After-Effects are extremely convenient whenever I get the urge to create a short film.
Sweet Dreams Are Made of Weaves
Ah yes, Adobe Dreamweaver, my newest side project. Without a doubt, learning HTML and CSS has been one of the most difficult tasks I've ever taken on. I have always been fascinated with coding languages, but also intimidated beyond belief. Using my precious summer days to memorize tags by writing endless strings in TextWrangler was a difficult ordeal, but I am extremely grateful that I did it. While I've only been using Dreamweaver for the past six months, the program has drastically increased by comprehension of HTML and CSS. Out of my entire set of skills, I believe that learning to format, design, and create web pages will provide the biggest payoff in the years to come.
From Pixels to Press
It's a common fact that our skills slowly build and branch as we take on new opportunities. That being said, I believe it's important to never forget the practices that kickstarted my passion for design in the first place. In the very beginning, my crude drawings were often printed onto shirts, hats, and stickers. As per the usual at my high school, if you spent time creating a design, it was your responsibility to operate the machines that would transform it into a product. After four years of burning shirts and acquiring numerous ink stains, I would consider myself pretty
good at operating your standard sublimation, flexographic, or screen-press printer.
